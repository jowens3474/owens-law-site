## STATEMENT OF THE CASE

**Nature of the case.** This is an appeal from a judgment of involuntary dismissal
entered under Mississippi Rule of Civil Procedure 41(b) at the close of the
plaintiff's case in a bench trial. Appellant Joanne Nelson sued to quiet title and
for a declaratory judgment of adverse possession to the parcel containing her
family home (Parcel 225), together with claims for injunctive relief, trespass,
conversion, and damages, against JJM Holdings, LLC and Josh Matthews, who acquired
a competing record title in November 2024 and gutted the home. (R. 445.)

**Course of proceedings and disposition.** Nelson tried her case over a day and a
half on September 15–16, 2025, presenting eight witnesses and numerous exhibits;
the chancellor also conducted a site visit on August 29, 2025. (R. 445–446.) At the
close of Nelson's case, the defendants moved for involuntary dismissal under Rules
41(b) and 52(a). (R. 445.) The chancellor granted the motion, entering a Judgment of
Involuntary Dismissal on October 17, 2025 (R. 424), and a Judgment of Supplemental
and Amended Involuntary Dismissal on October 20, 2025 (R. 445). The court found that
Nelson proved the adverse-possession elements of *claim of ownership, actual or
hostile,* and *peaceful* possession, but failed to prove *visible, continuous,* and
*exclusive* possession by clear and convincing evidence. (R. 447.) Nelson moved for
reconsideration and a new trial; the court denied the motion in a Final Judgment
entered November 24, 2025. (R. 498.) Nelson timely appealed. (R. ___ [verify].)

## STATEMENT OF FACTS

### A. The Nelson family home.

Joanne Nelson was born in Oxford in 1965, the only child of Joe Willie and Marie
Nelson. (Tr. 282–283; R. 447.) Her parents were sharecroppers; after her father died
when she was six, her mother bought a house and had it moved onto the family land on
Campground Road (County Road 217), where Joanne grew up. (Tr. 283.) That house — the
disputed property, assessed as Parcel 136W-13-225.00 ("Parcel 225") — adjoins the
family's undisputed parcel (Parcel 226). (R. 446–447.) Marie Nelson lived in and
occupied the home from the 1970s until her death on January 20, 2013. (R. 447–448,
451.) Joe Willie Nelson had died intestate on August 27, 1994. (R. 447.)

### B. Joanne Nelson's continuing possession after 2013.

Although she had moved to Jackson for work, Nelson maintained her possession of the
home. She testified that she came home "at least once a month," "[s]ometimes ...
every other week," and that the practice "pretty much ha[d] continued the same"
after her mother's death: "I come home. I check on her house. I check on my property.
I always go by her house. I go in her house. I walk around her house ... inside it."
(Tr. 300.) The electric service — in Marie Nelson's name since it was first connected
on August 4, 1975 — remained on until the defendants disconnected it after their 2024
purchase. (R. 448, 452.) Marie Nelson had claimed the homestead exemption on the
home; that exemption was removed in 2014, following her death, and Joanne Nelson
continued to pay the ad valorem taxes "up until present." (Tr. 175.)

### C. The defendants' competing title rests on a 1980 tax deed Nelson contends is void.

The defendants' record title traces to a tax deed from the Chancery Clerk of
Lafayette County to Wilbrod St. Amand, dated October 3, 1980 (Ex. 5) — an instrument
Nelson contends is void for want of a sufficient legal description. (R. 449.) The
chancellor nonetheless held the deed "only voidable" and found its defective
description "cured" by parol evidence — the testimony of the defendants' title
searcher, Matthew Moore, and of the Tax Assessor, Rocky Kennedy, together with
extrinsic documents such as assessment rolls and a published Notice to Creditors.
(R. 449.) From the St. Amand estate, the property was sold to HFVE Farms, LLC for
$20,100 (Exs. 3, 8), which conveyed to JJM Holdings, LLC by warranty deed dated
November 21, 2024, using a metes-and-bounds description drawn from a 2024 survey.
(R. 450.)

### D. The 2024 deed — not Nelson's possession — moved the home from Parcel 226 to Parcel 225 on the tax rolls.

The single fact the chancellor treated as fatal — that Nelson's taxes and homestead
were recorded on Parcel 226, not the disputed Parcel 225 — was itself a product of
the 2024 transaction Nelson challenges. The Tax Assessor, Rocky Kennedy (called by
Nelson), testified that the home had long been assessed on Parcel 226 and that the
residence was "transferred" and "moved" on the tax rolls from Parcel 226 to Parcel
225 only "upon that 2024 deed transfer." (Tr. 160–162.) When counsel asked whether
"a residence that was previously assessed to 226 [was] being moved to 225," the
chancellor interjected, "The answer to your question is yes." (Tr. 161.) Kennedy
confirmed that "another residence ... was moved from 226 on to 225 ... according to
the tax records," and that, had it not been, "there would currently be two residences
on 226." (Tr. 172.) The chancellor characterized the change as one of "assessment"
rather than a physical relocation of the house. (Tr. 176.) Thus, throughout the
decades of the Nelsons' possession, the home sat on the Parcel 226 assessment — where
Marie Nelson held her homestead and where the Nelsons paid taxes — and the Parcel 225
assignment was a 2024 bookkeeping change keyed to the very deed Nelson contends is
void. (Tr. 160–162, 170–176.)

### E. The defendants entered and gutted the home.

After purchasing in November 2024, Josh Matthews and JJM entered the home and gutted
it. Matthews testified that the defendants "jack[ed] the entire house up," "removed
the floor," "removed a lot of the walls," and "threw everything inside the house into
dumpsters." (Tr. 262.) Matthews acknowledged that he "never stepped foot into the
house until after" the work began. (Tr. 261.) Nelson's pleaded claims for trespass,
conversion, and damages arose from this entry and destruction. (R. 445.)
